About Me

I recently graduated from University of Washington in Seattle, with a Bachelors in History. I didn’t originally start out to get a degree in History, I was originally interested in pursuing computer science. At Edmonds community college I had taken all the calculus series classes they offered plus a few extra to prepare for this choice. However, after a few Intro to Java courses at Edmonds Community College and one at UW I found I didn’t enjoy the syntactically strict algorithmic backend language.

I stumbled into love with History after taking a Colonial History class my first quarter at UW. It wouldn’t be until a few quarters later that I truly fell in love with the research process. In a class at UW called The City we had an end of quarter project where we created a website based off our research of a block on the Seattle Waterfront. I had the blocks between Blanchard and Lenora. I spent months pouring over old Seattle newspapers, insurance maps, going through the UW digital archives, reading articles and visiting the current site. The project gave me a deeper understanding of Washington State and the city of Seattle. I caught the research bug with that project. My interest in research expanded with a research paper for a class on Postwar Japan. We got to pick the topic to research with professor’s approval. I researched Japans pop culture mediums before, during and after World War II. Not only did I learn how politics influences pop culture, I also learned the power pop culture has over people’s perception. As an enthusiast in pop culture this was a very impactful research project for me.

As I got more ingrained in history, I started to look for opportunities to continue exploring history and historical research outside of school. I found myself gravitating toward Archives and Museums. I started volunteering at National Archives and Records Administration and Museum of Pop Culture to help me get a better understanding of what they do and how they operate. Volunteering at National Archives and Records Administration gave me a new and interesting way to learn. For my first project I was tasked with indexing the McNeil Penitentiary prisoner intake forms. I would take the 1940’s handwritten inmate entries by the warden and translate them into an excel document. I learned a few interesting federal crimes while entering the inmates into the database, Selective Service and Training Act [Draft Dodging], N.M.V.T.A [Stolen Vehicle Trafficking]and White Slave Act [Human Trafficking] to name a few. It was an incredibly fascinating way to learn history.

I chose to volunteer at the Museum of Pop Culture because of my deep love of pop culture. I wanted to take the opportunity to learn how museums operated and where I would fit into the team, being surrounded by iconic pop culture objects was a bonus. It was during a volunteer event tour to the MoPop vaults, that I found a deep fascination with the collection’s aspect of museums. They wouldn’t let us raid the vaults of course but they pulled a few things to give us a taste of what was hidden in their massive collection. I was hooked and so became my interest in pursuing a degree in Museology so that I could be the one to interact with history and share it in new and interesting ways.

When my volunteering at MoPop ended I found an opportunity to internship at the Washington State Archive in Bellevue. I volunteered for a few weeks before starting the internship but with the rise of the Covid-19 pandemic I was only able to intern for two weeks before it was put on hold.

With my internship on hold and heading to cancellation I took the opportunity during the pandemic to learn web development. I was apprehensive as my last foray into coding hadn’t been the best experience for me but I was willing to give this new language a try. I found web development coding to be more interesting and more approachable. The visual aspect of it playing into a large part of why I like it so much. With pure back end language, it was hard for me to “see” what my code was doing, but with front end language I can see immediately what my code is doing in the browser. Which makes it easier for me to understand and troubleshoot. It also provided me more opportunity to express myself creatively, which I didn’t feel I could do with Java.

I want to combine my love of history and research with the web development I am learning and implementing. During my web dev class at UW I started this marriage by creating a simple history quiz that can be found under the history tab on this website that I also created from scratch. Even though the class is over I am still learning and expanding my understanding of web development. I’m using this website as a platform to experiment and grow my understanding.
